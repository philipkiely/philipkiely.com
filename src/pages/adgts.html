A Dangerous Guide to Security

In my first year of writing technical content, I reached hundreds of thousands of readers around the world, met some of my role models, and made thousands of dollars. A Dangerous Guide to Security will help you on your way to doing the same.


Earn money, make connections, and get noticed.

I earn $250 to $500 per article when writing tutorials — and with a few projects, that money adds up. But money isn't the only reason to write. A portfolio of published writing can give you a leg up toward earning a raise, landing a new job, or reaching new clients or customers.


Practice a proven writing method.

A Dangerous Guide to Security outlines my own method for writing and developing technical articles that will earn you money and look great in a portfolio. By following the principles in this book, you will become a better writer — and practicing quality techniques like these will speed up your rate of improvement.


Level up your skills, wherever you use them.

And regardless of why you write, this guide aims to provide you with the techniques you will need to reach your goals.

What's in a Dangerous Guide?




Intuitive Explanations

The handbook walks step-by-step through the process of deciding to write a cold email, figuring out who to email, finding their contact information, writing a compelling first message, and closing the conversation.

Illustrations

Go behind the scenes of my cold email success. Each of the nine examples includes one or two emails, context, and the payoff. I go through each email line-by-line to discuss why specific phrases and tactics were effective.

Clear Summaries

Forget hour-long slideshows with a talking head in the corner. A dozen tightly-paced videos cover a single topic each in four minutes or less. Watch them all in order over lunch or reference them as needed while working.

Artifacts

Develop your cold email skills with thirty days of guided exercises. Build your systems, complete writing prompts, and, of course, send plenty of cold emails. Complete the workbook at your own pace or over a structured month.

Questions?


Who is A Dangerous Guide to Security written for?

You, I hope! I originally imagined A Dangerous Guide to Security reaching an audience of project managers, team leads, small business owners, and anyone else who isn't a developer but works with them regularly. Basically, I want to help people who tell developers what to build understand what they are asking for. But, software developers without a background in security have also found the book useful.


How much programming experience do I need to understand this book?

No programming experience is required to read the book. While developers may benefit from the intuitive explainations in A Dangerous Guide to Security, the issues do not have any code in them nor do they assume any programming knowledge. The guide is written for smart professionals and assumes some level of digital literacy, but does not require or teach coding.

If enough people express interest, I will figure out how to make one available.

Because of the number of images, each issue of A Dangerous Guide to Security is only available in the PDF format.
